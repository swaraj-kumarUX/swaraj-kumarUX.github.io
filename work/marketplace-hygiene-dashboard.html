01 · Context & problem

Operators were being penalised
for problems they couldn't see

redBus flags underperforming routes and applies penalties that can pull a service off the marketplace. But the operator running that route had no way to see trouble coming. The metrics that decided their fate — cancellation rate, regularity index — lived in back-end reports they never opened, with no trend, no benchmark, and no signal of what to fix first.

The result was a trust problem dressed up as a data problem: operators felt punished by a black box, churned after surprise penalties, and flooded support with "why was my route flagged?" tickets.

"I only find out there's a problem when redBus emails me. By then I've already lost the route."

Operator · Tier-2 city · 8 routes
The constraints that made it hard
  • No real-time data. The pipeline refreshed every 4 hours — "live" was off the table for v1.
  • Design-system only. Had to ship on HubbleUI components — no custom widgets in v1.
  • Commercially sensitive. Couldn't expose absolute passenger numbers, only relative positions.
  • Tablet-first reality. Many operators run their office on a 10" Android tablet, not a desktop.

03 · Discovery

Three findings that
changed the design

I ran 6 operator interviews and audited 340 flagging-related tickets. Most of it confirmed the obvious. These three didn't — and each one killed an idea I'd have otherwise shipped.

Mental model

Operators think in routes, not metrics. They ask "is Route 12 okay?" — never "what's my aggregate cancellation rate?"

Killed the single "health score" concept before I drew it.
Timing

They discover a problem only when the penalty email arrives — too late to act. High performers had quietly built their own Excel trackers to fill the gap.

Reframed the brief from "more data" to "earlier signal."
Context

A raw number meant nothing. The most-requested feature in every interview was "is this good or bad?" — a benchmark against similar operators.

Made peer benchmarking a non-negotiable, not a nice-to-have.
04 · Framing & strategy

The bet: don't show more —
show what's wrong, first

The reframe everything hinged on

The team read this as a reporting gap — operators need more data. Discovery said otherwise: it was a timing and framing gap. Operators needed earlier signals, in the route-level language they already used, with a clear next action.

Obvious read

Give operators a richer dashboard — every metric, every trend, all of it.

→
The bet I made

Surface only the routes at risk, benchmarked, each with one recommended fix. Demote everything else.

Four principles I designed against
01

Lead with what's wrong

Triage before overview. The first thing on screen is the route that needs action today — not a summary.

02

Speak in routes

Every object is a route the operator recognises, not an abstract platform metric.

03

Never a number alone

Every metric ships with its peer benchmark. Context is the product, not the raw value.

04

Every problem, a fix

A flag without a recommended action is just anxiety. Pair each one with a next step.

05 · Explorations & tradeoffs

Three mental models,
tested against real route counts

I chose triage-first over a dense table — because operators with 20+ routes lost the signal in a grid, and a summary score gave them anxiety with nowhere to act.

Rejected

A · Metric cards

Bet: a high-level health score first, then drill down.

Why not: the summary created anxiety with nowhere to go — operators wanted to act, not admire a score.

Rejected

B · Dense table

Bet: power users want every route in one scannable grid.

Why not: fine at 3–4 routes, useless at 20+. Column overload buried the one route that mattered.

Chosen

C · Triage-first

Bet: show what needs attention first, not everything equally.

Result: "this is exactly how I think about my routes." Status-first, benchmarked, one screen — it won on the first test.

06 · Solution

Each screen answers
one operator question

The final design isn't a gallery — every part earns its place by resolving a question operators actually asked in research.

Decision 01
Triage panel of flagged routes
01
Answers: "which route needs me today?"

Triage panel leads

Flagged routes surface at the top, split from healthy ones. Operators see what needs action before they see anything else — mirroring the mental model they described.

Decision 02
Benchmark bar — operator vs peer
02
Answers: "is this good or bad?"

Benchmark bars, not raw numbers

Every metric shows the operator's value against the peer benchmark for their tier and route type. Raw numbers meant nothing; the comparison is what made them act.

Decision 03
Action tray with the recommended fix
03
Answers: "so what do I do?"

One recommended action per flag

Each flagged metric surfaces a single recommended fix. It removed the cognitive load of figuring out next steps — and tested far better than linking out to a help article.

Design-system contribution

Built entirely on HubbleUI v2. The benchmark comparison needed something the library didn't have, so I designed a new "benchmark" variant of the DataCard and raised it to the HubbleUI backlog — now reusable across the operator portal.

07 · Validation & impact

Tested honestly,
then shipped

Four operators, three rounds. Each round changed the design in a way I can point to.

Round 1 · Internal review

Cut the metric set from 9 to 5 with the PM — removed vanity metrics operators couldn't act on.

Round 2 · Operator testing

Added benchmark comparison bars after 3 of 4 operators asked "is this number good or bad?" mid-task.

Round 3 · Engineering review

Changed the refresh label from "Live" to "Updated 4 hrs ago" once eng confirmed the pipeline limit — honesty over polish.

The honest limit

The 4-hour data lag means this is early warning, not real-time. It closed the "surprise penalty" gap, but a genuinely proactive nudge needs the predictive layer below — not yet shipped.

08 · Reflection

If I ran it again

What surprised me

I expected operators to want more data. They wanted less, better-timed. The biggest unlock wasn't adding metrics — it was removing four that created noise.

What I'd change

Test earlier. We spent a week on the dense-table direction that a single 30-minute session would have killed on day three.

What's next

Predictive flagging — a "you're trending toward a flag" warning 72 hours out. The data already exists in the pipeline; it's a framing and trust problem now, not a data one.
